My research investigates how mundane infrastructures — housing, transportation, and digital systems — produce and reinforce unequal realities. I draw on spatial analysis, GeoAI, ethnography, and cartography to examine how systemic injustices shape wellness and health outcomes, with the goal of informing more just policies and communities.

## Housing Security

Access to safe and affordable housing is a human right and a key determinant of health. This project maps the landscape of affordable housing in New Jersey to understand the drivers of housing insecurity, producing tangible policy outputs for communities. Work conducted at Rutgers University, funded by the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation.

## AI Ethics and Accessibility

Ability is shaped by human environments. This project reveals the geography of disability and accessibility and develops a personalizable routing application that accounts for individual movement needs. My current work builds the emerging field of **critical Disability GIS**.

## Plumbing Poverty

Plumbing poverty is a conceptual and methodological tool for understanding the intersection of inequality, infrastructure, and space. This project mapped hot spots of racial and geographic inequality in U.S. household water insecurity, revealing stark disparities — particularly among Native American communities — that challenge assumptions about baseline infrastructure access in America.

**Publications**
- Deitz, Shiloh, and Katie Meehan. 2019. "Plumbing Poverty: Mapping Hot Spots of Racial and Geographic Inequality in U.S. Household Water Insecurity." *Annals of the American Association of Geographers.* <https://doi.org/10.1080/24694452.2018.1530587>
